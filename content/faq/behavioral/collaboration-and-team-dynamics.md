## 6) Tell me about a time you influenced without formal authority.

Strategic answer:
At Starbucks and Oracle, I often influenced by making tradeoffs explicit and showing implementation paths that de-risked execution. Rather than arguing from opinion, I used concrete options, impact, and sequencing to align teams. This was especially effective in cross-team settings where timelines were tight and ownership boundaries were complex. Influence came from clarity and follow-through.

## 7) Describe a time you mentored engineers.

Strategic answer:
A recurring pattern in my teams has been improving engineering fundamentals through practical coaching. At Oracle, I mentored engineers on unit testing discipline, dependency injection practices, and clearer PR communication so intent and risk were visible early. The value was not just cleaner code in one sprint, but a repeatable quality bar for future changes. I treat mentoring as a force multiplier for delivery.

## 9) Describe a conflict with a teammate or partner and how you handled it.

Strategic answer:
In cross-functional migrations, conflicts usually come from different risk tolerances rather than bad intent. My approach is to separate goals from implementation preferences, then align on decision criteria: customer impact, reversibility, and operational risk. Once criteria are explicit, disagreement becomes a design discussion instead of a personal one. This keeps teams constructive while still moving quickly.

## 15) Describe a time you improved team processes.

Strategic answer:
I helped improve team process quality by emphasizing clearer pull request intent, stronger test expectations, and consistent dependency management practices. In practice, this reduced avoidable review churn and made security/compliance upkeep less reactive. Process work is valuable when it removes friction from engineering flow rather than adding ceremony.

## 16) Tell me about working with stakeholders.

Strategic answer:
I work with stakeholders by translating technical decisions into impact, risk, and timing. In migration programs, that means giving clear progress markers, known risks, and mitigation options rather than overpromising certainty. Stakeholders usually respond well when they can see both the plan and the contingency plan.
